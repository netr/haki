      <Front>What is bikeshedding? (noun)</Front>
      <Back>The act of spending excessive time and energy on trivial details while neglecting more important issues.<br><br><div><strong>Example:&nbsp;</strong>During the meeting, the team spent hours <strong>bikeshedding</strong> about the color of the new logo instead of focusing on the marketing strategy.</div><div><br></div><div><strong>Origin/Etymology:&nbsp;</strong>The term originates from <strong>Parkinson's Law of Triviality</strong>, described by <strong>Cyril Northcote Parkinson</strong> in 1957. He illustrated it with the example of a committee spending disproportionate time debating the design of a bike shed while overlooking critical aspects of a nuclear power plant.</div><div><br></div><div><strong>Mnemonic Device:&nbsp;</strong><em>"Don't build a bike shed while the power plant remains unfinished."</em></div><div><em><br></em></div><div><strong>Related Terms:</strong></div><ul><li><strong>Triviality:</strong> Excessive focus on insignificant details.</li><li><strong>Nitpicking:</strong> Overly critical focus on minor faults.</li><li><strong>Scope Creep:</strong> Gradual focus drift from important objectives to minor details.</li></ul></Back>

      <Front>What is andragogy? (noun)</Front>
      <Back>The method and practice of teaching adult learners, emphasizing self-direction, experience, and practical application, in contrast to pedagogy, which focuses on teaching children.<br><br><div><strong>Example:&nbsp;</strong>A corporate training program where employees select modules aligned with their career goals and current responsibilities.</div><div><br></div><div><strong>Key Principles:</strong></div><ul><li><strong>Self-Directed Learning:</strong> Adults prefer to take responsibility for their own learning.</li><li><strong>Experience:</strong> Adults bring prior experiences that enrich the learning process.</li><li><strong>Relevance:</strong> Learning should directly relate to their personal or professional lives.</li><li><strong>Problem-Centered:</strong> Adults are driven to learn when it helps solve real-world problems.</li></ul><div><strong>Origin/Etymology:&nbsp;</strong>The term <em>andragogy</em> was popularized by <strong>Malcolm Knowles</strong> in the mid-20th century, deriving from the Greek words <em>andr-</em> (man/adult) and <em>agogos</em> (leader/teacher).</div><div><br></div><div><strong>Mnemonic Device:&nbsp;</strong><em>"Andragogy is adult-focused learning: Autonomous, Needs-based, Driven by relevance."</em></div><div><em><br></em></div><div><strong>Related Terms:</strong></div><ul><li><strong>Pedagogy:</strong> The method and practice of teaching children.</li><li><strong>Heutagogy:</strong> Self-determined learning, often beyond structured environments.</li></ul></Back>

      <Front>What is a portmanteau? (noun)</Front>
      <Back>A linguistic blend of words, where parts of multiple words are combined into a new word. It often combines the sounds and meanings of the original words.<br><br><b>Usage:</b>&nbsp;Portmanteaus are commonly used in everyday language to create new terms that are concise and descriptive.<br><br><div><strong>Example:</strong></div><ul><li><strong>Brunch:</strong> From "breakfast" and "lunch"</li><li><strong>Smog:</strong> From "smoke" and "fog"</li><li><strong>Motel:</strong> From "motor" and "hotel"</li></ul><div><strong>Origin/Etymology:</strong><br>The term <em>portmanteau</em> comes from the French word <em>portemanteau</em>, meaning <em>a suitcase</em>. It was popularized by Lewis Carroll in <em>Through the Looking-Glass</em> (1871) to describe blended words like <em>slithy</em> (slimy + lithe).</div><div><br></div><div><strong>Mnemonic Device:</strong><br><em>"A portmanteau is like a suitcase of words – it packs two meanings into one word."</em></div><div><em><br></em></div><div><strong>Related Terms:</strong></div><ul><li><strong>Compound Word:</strong> A word formed by combining two full words (e.g., "toothbrush").</li><li><strong>Blend Word:</strong> Another term for a portmanteau.</li></ul></Back>

      <Front>What is pidgin? (noun)</Front>
      <Back><div>A simplified form of language that develops as a means of communication between speakers of different native languages. It typically borrows elements from multiple languages and is used for specific purposes, such as trade or work.<br><br><b>Example:</b> The workers communicated using a pidgin that combined elements of English, Spanish, and local dialects.<br><br></div><div><strong>Origin/Etymology:&nbsp;</strong>The term <em>pidgin</em> originated from the <strong>Chinese Pidgin English</strong> in the <strong>19th century</strong>, where "pidgin" was derived from the word <strong>"business."</strong></div><div><b><br></b><div><strong>Mnemonic Device:&nbsp;</strong><em>"Pidgin is like a bridge language – simple, temporary, and built from parts of other languages."</em></div></div><div><strong><br></strong></div><div><strong>Related Terms:</strong><ul><li><strong>Dialect:</strong> A regional variant of a language.</li><li><strong>Jargon:</strong> Specialized terms used by a particular group.</li></ul></div></Back>
